# Creation Beyond The Classroom
When it comes to software engineering skills, it is important to recognize the grand history of engineering and architecture that has long since predated the digital space. Integral to understanding this process is a sense of wonder and curiosity about the things around you. Supplementary to this, however, is understanding the universal aspects that carry themselves across the wide variety of contexts in which things are made, improved, and altered upon. Software engineering has a few critical examples of ideas that move past the field itself into the wider arena of design itself. Namely, these are the coding standards and design patterns themselves that have various analogues to compare to. The process of standardization and building upon what has come before is something that is useful in nearly every field - especially electrical engineering and circuit design (a field very distinct from designing web applications).

# Standards and Conventions
On the topic of circuit design, there are various writing conventions that are simply taken as a given when reading a schematic as they streamline the process of analysis. Current flows from positive to negative, all voltages are referenced to ground, and ideal sources have no internal resistance. These are ideas that not only are simplifying assumptions that make schematics easier to understand, but they also allow us to decompose problems into ideal and non-ideal parts. An example of this is being able to decompose a given source into its ideal source alongisde its internal resistance, capacitance, and inductance. Similarly, coding standards make programs that much easier to understand by outlining the uses of different data such as local, global, and constant variables, or by avoiding implicit types when engaging in a functional program. These help the developer avoid undefined behavior while better capturing the intent of the program. Separately of course, we are also concerned with the documentation that avoids reinventing the wheel.

# The Wheel We Always Use
In circuit design and programming alike, we might want to annotate and generally keep in mind the design patterns that we are using. It's important to not do excess work when not needed, and it is always a plus when we can rely on the ideas of greater thinkers before us. In circuit design, the various parameters of a given transistor or diode have all been tested by a manufacturer under various conditions, and so there is no need to infer these internal values from circuit analysis or digital simulation. In any case, we might also rely on the various designs of the past such as using an existing power converter in an automobile context as much work has already been done in the sector. Separately, we see the same patterns arise in programming. When storing, searching, and filtering data, there are numerous algorithms that have long since been designed to do this effectively with both time and memory. Inevitably, it becomes a waste of time (and an alluring one at that) to reinvent the wheel. But as engineers, it's our prerogative to standardize and rely upon the wheel we always use.
